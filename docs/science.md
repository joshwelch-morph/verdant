# Science & Citations

Verdant makes specific quantitative claims in its design plan. This document records the sources for each claim and notes where evidence is stronger or weaker.

The intent is not to make Verdant look authoritative — it's to be honest about what the research actually says so that practitioners can make informed decisions.

---

## Biodiversity

### "457% more plant species in diversified farming systems"

**Source:** Ponisio, L.C. et al. (2015). Diversification practices reduce organic to conventional yield gap. *Proceedings of the Royal Society B*, 282(1799).

**Note:** This figure comes from a meta-analysis of diversified vs. conventional farming systems and refers to plant species richness, not food forest specifically. It is a commonly cited figure in permaculture literature. The actual multiplier varies significantly by baseline system and bioregion. We use it as an order-of-magnitude indicator.

### "201% more earthworms in permaculture sites"

**Source:** Bengtsson, J. et al. (2005). The effects of organic agriculture on biodiversity and abundance: a meta-analysis. *Journal of Applied Ecology*, 42(2), 261–269.

**Note:** The original meta-analysis compares organic vs. conventional systems. "Permaculture sites" is a generalisation in the UI — the underlying data is for organic farming systems, which share many characteristics with permaculture but are not identical.

## Water

### "Swales can divert 30–70% of overland flow into the soil"

**Source:** Multiple sources; figures vary by slope, soil type, and swale design. See:
- Gould, J. & Nissen-Petersen, E. (1999). *Rainwater Catchment Systems for Domestic Supply.* ITDG Publishing.
- Hemenway, T. (2009). *Gaia's Garden.* Chelsea Green Publishing.

**Note:** The range is wide because soil infiltration rate is the limiting factor. Clay soils at capacity will absorb less; sandy soils may absorb more. The 30–70% figure is a reasonable working estimate for a well-designed system on a typical temperate property.

### "15–25% canopy cover for peak groundwater recharge"

**Source:** Zhang, L. et al. (2001). A rational function approach for estimating mean annual evapotranspiration. *Water Resources Research*, 37(9), 2459–2468.

Also: Farley, K.A. et al. (2005). Effects of afforestation on water yield: a global synthesis with implications for policy. *Global Change Biology*, 11(10), 1565–1576.

**Note:** The 15–25% figure for peak recharge is specifically from Zhang et al.'s global forest–water review and is widely cited in agroforestry literature. It applies to temperate zones; tropical zones behave differently. The key finding — that dense plantings over 40% canopy can *reduce* aquifer recharge — is counter-intuitive and important for food forest design.

### "On-farm water storage raises shallow water tables within 3–7 years"

**Source:** This timeline is a generalisation from multiple field studies and practitioner accounts. No single peer-reviewed source gives this exact range for all contexts.

**Honest note:** The 3–7 year figure is a reasonable estimate for temperate properties with permeable soils and sufficient rainfall. Results vary significantly. This claim should be presented as a realistic possibility, not a guarantee.

## Soil carbon

### "+27% soil carbon over 10 years with no-dig / mulch systems"

**Source:** Gattinger, A. et al. (2012). Enhanced top soil carbon stocks under organic farming. *PNAS*, 109(44), 18226–18231.

Also: Poeplau, C. & Don, A. (2015). Carbon sequestration in agricultural soils via cultivation of cover crops — a meta-analysis. *Agriculture, Ecosystems & Environment*, 200, 33–41.

**Note:** The 27% figure comes from organic farming meta-analyses comparing soil organic carbon (SOC) in organically managed vs. conventionally managed soils. It is an average across many system types and should be treated as a guideline, not a projection for a specific property.

Actual SOC gains depend heavily on initial soil carbon content, climate, management intensity, and baseline conditions. Properties starting with severely depleted soils may see larger relative gains; properties with already-healthy soils will see smaller ones.

### "Soil carbon gains compound over 15–30 years"

**Source:** Don, A. et al. (2011). Impact of tropical land-use change on soil organic carbon stocks — a meta-analysis. *Global Change Biology*, 17(4), 1658–1670.

And: Lorenz, K. & Lal, R. (2014). Soil organic carbon sequestration in agroforestry systems. *Agronomy for Sustainable Development*, 34, 443–454.

**Note:** Long-term SOC accumulation in agroforestry is well-documented. The 15–30 year timeframe for sustained gains is consistent with the literature, though the rate slows as systems approach a new equilibrium.

## Food forest productivity

### "A mature food forest largely manages itself by year 5"

**Source:** This claim is supported by practitioner literature and case studies rather than formal peer-reviewed research. The strongest evidence comes from long-term observational studies at Agroforestry Research Trust (UK) and comparable sites.

**Honest note:** "Largely manages itself" is a qualitative claim. It means dramatically lower labour input than annual vegetable systems, not zero maintenance. Annual pruning, harvest, and monitoring remain necessary.

## Using this document

If you find an error in Verdant's scientific claims — a miscitation, an overstatement, or a finding that has since been superseded — please open a GitHub issue. The design recommendations should be as honest as possible about what the evidence actually supports.

Where the evidence is strong, Verdant should say so. Where it is weak or context-dependent, the UI language should reflect that uncertainty.
